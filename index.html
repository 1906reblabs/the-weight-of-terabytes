Chapter One
The Load-Shedding Hours
"A man who does not build will always be building."
I. The Load-Shedding Hours

The darkness came at six, same as always. Langa Ndimande didn't bother with candles anymore. He'd learned to work in the window — that forty-minute seam of amber Highveld light before the East Rand swallowed the sun whole — fingers moving over a keyboard that had lost its 'S' key three months ago.

He typed anyway. A missing key was nothing compared to what he'd already lost.

Outside, Brakpan settled into its dusk ritual. Somewhere down Van Riebeeck Avenue, a neighbour fired up a generator that rattled like a man with a chest infection. Kids played in the street, their laughter bouncing off old face-brick walls, off the fever trees that had cracked the pavements years ago and never been repaired. The municipality had forgotten this suburb the way it forgot most things — bills, maintenance, the future. Langa had made peace with that forgetting. It was the only mercy Ekurhuleni had ever shown him.

He hit save. Thirty-two thousand rows of JSE-listed equity data, cleaned, normalised, tagged. His salvation. Maybe.

II. What the Courts Recorded

The liquidation papers had said R4.7 million. That was the number. Not the faces of the investors — his mother's church friends, his former university classmates, a cousin who had trusted him across a WhatsApp message — but the number. Courts preferred numbers.

Langa had believed in the algorithm. He'd read every Investopedia article, every quant finance thread on Reddit at 2am, every Medium post by some American in a Patagonia vest who'd turned forty thousand dollars into a lifestyle brand. He had built a dashboard that looked like Bloomberg Terminal if you squinted and believed in yourself hard enough. His investors had believed. Zanele had believed, at first.

She'd stopped believing on a Wednesday. He remembered because it was bin day, and she'd placed her wedding ring in the recycling by mistake, and when he found it gleaming between a flattened Oros carton and a yoghurt lid, she said: Keep it. It's probably worth more than our joint account.

She took Amahle and Sibusiso to her mother in Soweto. The baby — Langa still called him the baby even though Sibusiso was now seven — had waved from the back window of the Uber. That wave had restructured something inside Langa's chest. It hadn't healed right.

III. The Tutoring Kingdom

Every Saturday morning, eight children arrived at the house on Sarel Cilliers Street. Their mothers sat in cars outside scrolling phones while Langa taught maths and science at a plastic table in the lounge, a whiteboard propped against the fireplace. He charged six hundred rand per child per month. He paid nothing for electricity. Nothing for water. The municipality's system had flagged his account in a backlog queue that hadn't been processed since the Zuma years, and Langa had stopped waiting for the letter that would change that.

Four thousand eight hundred rand a month. After food, after airtime, after the two hundred rand he sent Zanele when he could, it bought him approximately ninety gigabytes of data.

Ninety gigabytes was not nothing when you were building a dataset.

He'd started with what he knew: JSE trade records, scraped carefully, cleaned obsessively. Then macroeconomic indicators — CPI, repo rate movements, rand-dollar daily closes going back fifteen years. Then the thing he was most proud of: sentiment tagging on SENS announcements, the regulatory filings that listed companies published before major price movements. He had read four thousand eight hundred and twelve announcements by hand. He had tagged each one. Positive. Negative. Neutral. Material. Speculative. His eyes had deteriorated two prescription levels in eight months.

His optometrist, Dr. Ferreira on Voortrekker Road, had mentioned this with mild concern.

"What are you doing that keeps you reading so much?"

"Building something," Langa had said.

Dr. Ferreira had nodded the way men nod when they have decided not to ask the next question.

IV. Fine-Tuning Dreams

He had no GPU. This was not, strictly speaking, a problem yet — training the model would require hardware that cost more than his house. Preparing the data required only time. And time was the one resource that poverty had accidentally given him in abundance.

The irony was not lost on him. He had once moved other people's money through systems he barely understood, skimming confidence from YouTube tutorials and the liquidity-soaked rebound of late 2020 that briefly convinced half the country they understood markets. Now he moved data through systems he was beginning — slowly, with the particular hunger of a man who has nothing else — to actually understand.

He was building something specific: a financial reasoning dataset, South African in flavour, JSE-specific in its bones. The kind of dataset that a quant desk at Rand Merchant Bank or FirstRand would find useful. The kind that didn't exist yet because everyone building AI was building it for the S&P 500, for the Nasdaq, for markets where the volatility carried American accents.

He had written the pitch deck in his head seventeen times. He had not yet written it down. Writing it down made it vulnerable to being wrong.

V. The Night Architecture

At 9pm the street was quiet. Load-shedding had lifted and one bulb in the kitchen threw a yellow cone across the linoleum. Langa made two-minute noodles and ate standing at the counter, looking at nothing.

On his phone, a WhatsApp notification: Amahle drew a picture of you today. She says you have a computer instead of a face.

Zanele had sent a photo. His daughter — eight years old, all her mother's forehead and his ears — had drawn a man at a desk, his head replaced by a rectangle with lines on it. She had written DADDY in red crayon underneath, with a heart. Below the heart, in the smaller, uncertain script of a child still learning that letters could be permanent: come home soon.

Langa set the phone face-down. He sat for a moment with the weight of three words and a crayon heart. He picked the phone back up and looked at the drawing one more time — the rectangle head, the careful heart, the instruction disguised as a wish. Then he set it face-down again, more gently this time, the way you set down something you cannot afford to break further. He went back to the laptop.

The dataset had 47,000 rows now. He needed 200,000 for the pitch to be serious. He opened a new spreadsheet tab. He titled it: SENS_ANNOTATIONS_BATCH_24.

He worked until the load-shedding came back at midnight and took the light.

In the dark, he sat for a while without moving. Through the window, the East Rand sky was doing what it always did — that amber-grey wash from the mine dumps and the street lights and the distant glow of Johannesburg, which had never quite decided whether it was a city of endings or beginnings.

Langa thought: 200,000 rows. Then one email to the right address. Then everything changes.

He decided to believe it.

Outside, a dog barked twice and then the suburb was silent, and Langa Ndimande sat in the dark and waited for the power to come back, the way a man waits when he has learned that waiting is also a form of work.

"Langa means sun," his grandmother had told him when he was small, sitting in her kitchen in KwaMashu, the smell of pap and woodsmoke and certainty. "The thing that comes back every day, whether the world deserves it or not." He had forgotten this for a while. He was remembering now.

Chapter Two
The Analyst
"She had the quality of complete attention — which is rarer than beauty, and lasts longer."
I. The Email He Rewrote Eleven Times

He found her through LinkedIn at 1am on a Thursday. Her name was Nandi Dlamini. Senior Quantitative Analyst, Alternative Data & AI Strategy, Rand Merchant Bank. She had published two internal white papers on NLP applications in emerging market credit scoring, which RMB had been proud enough to post publicly. Langa had read both papers twice. Then a third time with a notebook.

She knew what he was building. She just didn't know she knew.

He drafted the email on his phone because the laptop battery had given up at midnight. The first draft was too eager. The second was too technical. The third sounded like a man who needed something, which was true but fatal. He rewrote it eight more times over the next twenty-four hours.

The eleventh version was this:

To:[email protected]
Subject:JSE-Specific Financial Reasoning Dataset — South African AI Fine-Tuning
Dear Ms. Dlamini,

I have spent the past eight months building a structured dataset for fine-tuning financial language models on JSE-listed instruments — 200,000 annotated rows covering SENS filings, equity price history, macro indicators and multi-label sentiment classification with a three-tier taxonomy: directional sentiment, materiality classification, and event class. All data is localised to the South African market context that offshore models consistently misread.

I noticed your work on NLP applications in EM credit. I think what I am building is the layer beneath what you are doing. I would welcome twenty minutes of your time.

Regards,
Langa Ndimande

He did not mention Brakpan. He did not mention the missing keyboard key. He did not mention that he had 47,000 rows, not 200,000.

He sent it at 6:17am on a Friday and then walked to the kitchen and stared at the kettle for a long time without turning it on.

II. She Replied on a Saturday

He had expected to wait until at least Monday; the corporate world usually ignored Brakpan on weekends. Waiting had a cost. Waiting meant that your survival was contingent on someone else's decision, and he had already paid that particular price in full.

So the notification found him mid-sentence during Saturday tutoring, a red marker in his hand. He felt his phone vibrate against his hip and did not look at it. He finished the correction. He explained the principle to a ten-year-old named Kagiso who understood it immediately and grinned with all her teeth. He collected his four thousand eight hundred rand in cash envelopes after the last child left.

He looked at his phone in the empty lounge, standing in the particular silence that houses hold after children have left them.

From:[email protected]
Subject:Re: JSE-Specific Financial Reasoning Dataset
Hi Langa,

Thank you for reaching out. The framing of your dataset is interesting — you are right that EM localisation is a persistent gap in available training corpora. I'd be open to a brief call to understand the methodology and scope better.

I'm available Wednesday at 2pm or Thursday at 10am.

Nandi

He read it three times. Then sat down on the floor because the couch had a broken spring he kept meaning to fix. He chose Wednesday. He would need Tuesday to prepare.

III. Wednesday at Two

Her video came on a few seconds after his. The RMB background was that particular shade of corporate warm-grey that said we have thought carefully about being approachable. She wore a navy blazer over a white shirt and no jewellery except small gold earrings, and she looked exactly like her photograph except more alive, which photographs always failed to account for.

"Langa," she said, and said his name correctly without asking, with the ease of someone who grew up in a house where names were not obstacles. "Thanks for making the time."

"Thank you for the reply," he said, and immediately moved past the pleasantries because pleasantries were where deals went to die. "I sent you a sample this morning. Five thousand rows, full data dictionary. I want to walk you through the annotation logic before you form an opinion about the methodology."

Something shifted in her face — a small recalibration, the kind that happens when someone arrives slightly different from how you'd prepared for them.

"I've already looked at it," she said. "The SENS sentiment taxonomy is interesting. Three-tier classification — most people building this kind of thing go binary."

"Binary loses the material-versus-speculative distinction," Langa said. "And in South African filings that distinction does a lot of work. A CEO change at a mid-cap mining house and a CEO change at a JSE-listed bank read the same to an offshore model. They are not the same event."

She looked at him for a moment. Not the polite attention of a professional meeting but actual looking, the kind that takes inventory.

"How many rows do you have in total?"

"Currently 61,000," he said. He had annotated fourteen thousand rows in the four days since her reply, sleeping in three-hour blocks, eating when he remembered to. "Target is 200,000. I'm on track to deliver a complete corpus in Q2."

It was not a lie. It was an extremely ambitious version of the truth.

After the call ended, Langa sat very still in the straight-backed chair for a long time. Then he opened a new terminal window and started annotating. Batch thirty-two was not going to annotate itself.

Interlude
Nandi Dlamini — A Portrait
"She did not enter rooms. She recalibrated them."
I. What Meadowlands Made

She had grown up on a street in Meadowlands, Zone 8, where the houses were small and the ambitions were enormous and the two things existed without apology in the same square kilometre. Her father, Bongani Dlamini, taught mathematics at Orlando West High School for thirty-one years — the same school where students had once marched into a future nobody had planned for them — and he had the particular gravity of men who know that the subject they teach is not the subject they are actually teaching. What he taught, always, was precision. The willingness to be exactly right, to do the work until the answer was not approximately correct but completely correct, which were two entirely different things.

Nandi had understood this at nine years old. By twelve she had moved past her father in algebra and neither of them mentioned it, which was its own kind of tenderness.

Her mother, Thandi, ran a small insurance brokerage from a converted garage at the side of the house. She was not a mathematician. She was something more durable: a woman who understood that money was a form of language, and that if you did not learn to speak it fluently, other people would always be translating for you, with their own interests in the margins. She had built her client base entirely on referrals, and her referrals were entirely built on the fact that she never made a promise she could not keep. In Meadowlands, this was a currency more reliable than the rand.

Nandi had absorbed both of them. She had her father's precision and her mother's read of a room. This was an unusual combination. Most people had one or the other.

II. The Wits Years, and What They Cost

She had gone to Wits on a merit bursary — applied mathematics and economics — and experienced the specific disorientation of being in a place that was theoretically built for everyone and practically built for a subset of everyone whose parents had been in the room when the blueprints were drawn. She was not the only student from Soweto. She was, in several of her third-year seminars, the only Black woman. She had learned not to perform either comfort or discomfort about this, because performing either was a form of concession she was not willing to make.

What she had found at Wits, beneath the disorientation, was the thing she had been moving towards her entire life without having a name for it: the point where mathematics and human behaviour intersected. Markets were not rational. She had known this the way children know things — by watching adults make choices that defied the logic they claimed to operate by. What she had not known, until Professor Amara Okafor introduced her to behavioural economics in her second year, was that the irrationality was structured. Predictable. Mappable. That you could build a model of human error with the same rigour you applied to any other system.

This had felt, to Nandi, like being handed the right tool for a job she had been attempting with her bare hands.

She wrote her Honours thesis on momentum anomalies in JSE small-cap stocks. Her examiners were impressed. One of them, a visiting professor from the University of Cape Town, said her methodology was "unusually mature." She had smiled and thanked him and spent the afternoon wondering what the "unusually" was doing in that sentence.

III. How She Worked

At RMB she had a reputation that preceded her through the building with the particular velocity of things that are both true and slightly simplified. The simplified version was: brilliant, difficult to read, always right about data. The true version was more interesting.

She was not difficult to read. She was simply not performing. Most professional environments ran on a low constant hum of performance — people signalling their comfort, their engagement, their openness to feedback. Nandi had no interest in the signal. She had interest in the substance. If you said something worth her attention, you received it completely. If you did not, her face gave you that information directly, and she had stopped apologising for this sometime around her second year at the bank.

She worked the way her father had taught quadratic equations — completely, to the last decimal place, until the answer was not approximately right but actually right. She had a notebook she called the shelf — a brown Moleskine she kept in her desk drawer — full of ideas not ready to be built yet but too precise to lose. She had been adding to the shelf for four years. She had built three things from it. She expected to build four more.

She read late at night. She ran on Sunday mornings in Emmarentia, early, before the park filled with people. Running was the one activity that did not require her to be excellent at it. She found this restful in a way that was difficult to explain to anyone who had spent their whole life being good at the things they did.

On the Question of Men

She had not been in a relationship for two years. The last had ended when her former partner, a corporate lawyer named Siyanda, had said: "You are always somewhere else even when you are here." She had sat with that sentence for a long time. She had not disputed it. What she had not been able to explain to him — what she was not sure she could explain to anyone — was that the somewhere else was not indifference. It was the ongoing hum of problems she was in the middle of solving. It was the habit of mind that her father had given her and that her work had amplified until it was simply the atmosphere she lived in. She was always in the middle of a calculation. This was not coldness. It was the specific temperature of someone whose whole life was thought.

She had not found a person yet who could be in that room with her and not find it lonely.

IV. What She Noticed About the Email

She received perhaps twelve cold emails a week from people with datasets, startups, ideas, pitches, and occasionally things they called "revolutionary frameworks" for understanding markets. She deleted most of them within four seconds. The deletion was not callousness. It was pattern recognition, which she trusted the way her father had trusted the answer at the bottom of a calculation: absolutely, until the working showed otherwise.

Langa Ndimande's email had stopped her deletion reflex for one specific reason. There was no desperation in it. Cold outreach was almost always structurally desperate — the subject line, the self-description, the pivot to the ask. His email had skipped the pivot. He had stated a fact, made a connection, and asked for twenty minutes. He had not told her what the dataset was worth. He had not told her how many potential clients he was in discussion with. He had not told her, in other words, any of the things that people told you when they were trying to manage your perception rather than share information.

She had opened the sample he sent the following morning and spent forty minutes with the data dictionary before their call. The three-tier sentiment taxonomy had been the thing. Not because it was clever — though it was — but because it revealed someone who had read the filings themselves, not just designed a system to classify them. You could not design that taxonomy from first principles alone. You had to have sat with the material until you understood what the material was actually doing.

She knew the difference. It was the difference between her father explaining a quadratic and a textbook explaining one.

After their first call, she had opened her brown notebook and written a single line on the last page: Langa Ndimande — the layer beneath. She did not know yet what that meant precisely. She had learned to trust that the shelf would tell her, eventually, if she was patient enough to wait for it.

Chapter Three
The Saturdays
"Community is what you have when you can no longer afford solitude."
I. The Bolt Philosophy

He did not own a car. This was not the East Rand way, where cars were a form of dignity and driving yourself was the first tier of proof that you had arrived somewhere. But Langa had liquidated his car eighteen months ago — a 2018 Polo, burgundy, one careful owner, which had been himself — and had discovered in its absence that Johannesburg was navigable by taxi and Bolt if you were willing to trade speed for the unexpected education of other people's windshields.

The drivers talked. This was the thing that surprised him, repeatedly, about moving through the East Rand on someone else's fuel. Drivers talked in the specific way of people who spent their working day in a confessional — except they were the ones confessing, because the passenger in the back seat was a stranger who would be gone in fourteen minutes and take whatever they'd said with them into the irretrievable past.

There was Mr. Khoza, sixty-one, former NUM shop steward at Ergo Mining before the retrenchments, who drove a white Corolla with a pine-tree air freshener and a discourse on post-industrial South Africa that could have doubled as a graduate seminar. "They told us the machines were coming," he said, once, watching the mine dump rise on the left side of the R51. "They did not tell us the machines would need people to teach them." He had looked at Langa in the rearview. "That is what you do, is it not?"

Langa had said: "Something like that."

There was Takudzwa, twenty-four, from Bulawayo, studying part-time at UNISA while driving. He kept his phone mounted on the dashboard with a secondary app running below the Bolt interface — a crypto portfolio tracker, green numbers flickering between rides. "I never have more than three hundred rand in it," he said when Langa asked. "I am learning how it moves. When I understand how it moves, I will put more." This was, Langa reflected, better risk management than he had applied to his own investors' R4.7 million.

And there was the driver — he never caught the name — who had looked at Langa through the mirror somewhere between Brakpan and Daveyton and said: "My nephew says you teach him maths on Saturdays. He says you explain things until they make sense, not just until the time is up." He had paused at a robot. "That is a scarce quality, mfowethu."

Langa had looked out the window at the East Rand scrolling past and thought: I used to think I was building a company. Perhaps I have only ever been doing this.

II. Thabiso's Internet Café

The internet café on the main road in Tsakane was called Cyber Zone, but everyone called it Thabiso's, which was more honest. Thabiso Sithole had opened it with a loan from his mother five years ago on the premise that internet access was infrastructure, not luxury — a premise that was entirely correct and, like most correct premises, had not been sufficient to make him rich. The business survived on printing, on PlayStation time rented by the hour to teenagers, and on the municipal Wi-Fi contract Thabiso had won by submitting a tender application that was, by his own account, "eighty percent my words and twenty percent words I borrowed from a sample tender I found on the government website."

The café had twelve computers, a laminator, a broken water cooler that had become a shelf for uncollected print jobs, and a handwritten sign on the wall that said LOAD SHEDDING SCHEDULE — below which Thabiso had written, in a different pen: SCHEDULE SUBJECT TO ESKOM'S MOOD.

Langa spent two Saturdays a month here after tutoring, when he needed bandwidth for the larger scraping jobs and the municipal Wi-Fi was faster than his phone hotspot. He had known Thabiso since secondary school — they had been in the same science class, where Thabiso had distinguished himself by being the only student who consistently failed the theory components while building more sophisticated experiments than the teacher had designed. He was, Langa had always thought, a man whose intelligence had been assigned the wrong assessment rubric.

"You're still at it," Thabiso said, watching Langa's download bar on a Saturday afternoon in March. Not a question.

"I'm close," Langa said.

"You've been close for six months."

"I'm closer."

Thabiso put a mug of Five Roses on the desk beside him and returned to the counter, where a teenage boy was arguing about PlayStation time with the intensity of someone negotiating peace terms. This was how Thabiso expressed support — tea, proximity, and the courtesy of not asking follow-up questions.

III. Mandla and Nomvula

Mandla Ngwenya sold refurbished phones from a table outside a tyre shop on the Daveyton side of things, and his relationship to risk was so fundamentally different from Langa's that they circled each other with the mutual fascination of people who have arrived at the same understanding of the world through entirely opposite routes.

Mandla bought broken phones from repair shops, fixed what could be fixed, sold what could be sold, and reinvested the margin before the rand had time to change its mind. His analysis was tactile, instinctive, completed in seconds. He held a phone and he knew. Langa had watched him do this and felt, each time, a complicated admiration — for the speed of it, and for what the speed required him not to know. Mandla's system worked precisely because he never paused long enough to doubt it.

On a Saturday in February, leaning against the tyre shop wall, Langa had tried to explain the dataset.

Mandla had listened with the expression of a man who was willing to be interested but needed evidence of a reason to be. When Langa finished, Mandla said: "So you're building something for people with money."

Langa had looked down at the pavement, at the dust and the old tyre marks and the singular ecology of a busy East Rand forecourt. Then he said, quietly: "No. I'm building something that might give me some."

Mandla had looked at him for a moment. Then he nodded — not the encouraging nod, not the sympathetic nod, but the specific nod of a man who has recognised the sentence another man has been waiting to say out loud. "Then build it fast," was all he said.

Nomvula Khumalo ran a catering operation from her home kitchen in Tsakane that supplied three office parks in Kempton Park, a monthly stokvels meeting in Boksburg, and two school tuck shops. She kept her books in a spreadsheet that was, by Langa's professional assessment, more rigorously structured than most of the investment dashboards he had built in his previous life. She had never taken an accounting course. She had taken a cash flow problem, once, and had solved it by calling twelve people in two hours and restructuring her payment terms across three clients in a single afternoon.

"You know what your problem is," she said to Langa one Saturday, distributing containers of food from the boot of her Nissan. "You built your first business for people who believed in you. Now you are building for people who don't know you exist." She handed him a container of pap and chicken. "That is harder, but it is more honest."

Langa ate standing up, leaning against the car, the East Rand afternoon wide and copper around him. He thought about that distinction for a long time afterward. Longer, perhaps, than any single insight from the ArXiv papers he had read.

Chapter Four
The Orthodox Problem
"The desert fathers built nothing visible. That was the point."
I. The Small Corner

In the corner of the room where the dataset lived — between the external hard drive and the wall where the plaster had a crack shaped vaguely like a river delta — Langa kept a shelf. On it were three things: a small icon of Christ Pantocrator, its gold background worn at the edges where the previous owner had touched it; a beeswax candle in a red glass holder; and a prayer book with a cracked spine and Post-it notes marking fasting regulations and evening prayers.

There was no Orthodox church in Brakpan. There was no Orthodox church in any reasonable travelling distance of Brakpan. The nearest was a small Greek parish in Johannesburg North that held services in Greek and English alternately, and which Langa had visited twice — once to stand in the back and understand what he was looking for, and once to speak to the priest, Father Konstantinos, a small soft-spoken man from Thessaloniki who had looked at him with neither surprise nor excessive welcome, which was itself a form of welcome Langa had not expected.

"You came from far," Father Konstantinos had said.

"I come from everywhere far," Langa had answered, and the priest had nodded with the comprehension of a man who understood that this was a theological statement, not a geographical one.

He could not come regularly. The taxi fare was a calculation he could not always make work. So he practiced where he was, with what he had, in the manner he had slowly learned — from books, from forums, from a Romanian priest who streamed his liturgies from a monastery outside Sibiu and whose comment section was an improbable community of converts on multiple continents, many of them as geographically isolated as Langa, all of them attending the same service through the same screen that also carried, in other tabs, their loneliness and their work and their ordinary lives.

II. Faith as Infrastructure

He had discovered Orthodoxy the way many modern converts did — through a late-night internet spiral that had begun with a search about Byzantine trade routes and ended, three hours later, on a YouTube channel run by a monk in Romania whose comment section was an improbable community of converts on multiple continents, all of them attending the same livestream liturgy through the same screen that also carried, in other tabs, their loneliness and their ordinary lives. He had watched the first video to the end. Then he had watched it again. The monks sang for hours in the dark for no audience, no analytics, no content strategy. They were doing the work because the work was the point.

This was, he had slowly understood, the only relationship to labour he trusted anymore. His first company had been built on visibility — the performance of competence, the Instagram of the entrepreneurial self, the LinkedIn post about the journey. The dataset was built in the opposite direction: inward, invisible, patient, without an audience, in the dark of Brakpan while the East Rand slept.

Faith, Langa had discovered, was easier to practice when nobody around you understood it. The absence of community removed the temptation to perform the faith rather than inhabit it.

He fasted on Wednesdays and Fridays, which in practical terms meant skipping breakfast and eating simply in the evening. The skipping of breakfast cost him nothing he could not afford to forgo. The simplicity of the evening meal was indistinguishable from his ordinary poverty. He was not sure whether this made the fasting more or less meaningful. He had decided it made it more so — that practicing an ascetic discipline when you are already materially thin was a different act than practicing it from abundance. It was the difference between choosing less and choosing less deliberately.

The icon watched the dataset from across the room. He had not arranged this with any particular intention. It had simply become the arrangement — the gold face of the Pantocrator looking at the spreadsheets, the annotated filings, the slow accumulation of a thing that might, in time, become a kind of salvation. He was not sure what the icon made of the dataset. He suspected it was not impressed by it in the way he needed it to be impressed. This was, possibly, the point of icons.

III. The Romanian Priest's Sermon

One Thursday night, in the amber light of a load-shedding reprieve, Father Gheorghe's livestream sermon translated haltingly through subtitles that were clearly the work of an AI model trained on formal theological language rather than pastoral speech. The priest was speaking about the concept of nepsis — watchfulness, sobriety of spirit, attention without anxiety.

The subtitle read: To be watchful is not to be vigilant in the sense of the fearful man. It is to be awake to the present moment with full understanding, without grasping for the next moment or mourning the last.

Langa had read this sentence three times. Then he had minimised the livestream tab and opened his annotation spreadsheet. He thought about the distinction between watchfulness and anxiety for the rest of that session, working through batch thirty-five, and he understood something he could not quite articulate about why he had been able to sit in the dark of the dataset for eight months without collapsing — when he had been unable, before, to sit inside failure for eight days without making a decision he would regret.

The difference was not hope. He had had hope before, vast and reckless and borrowed from other men's success stories. The difference was something quieter. The willingness to be where he actually was, doing what he was actually doing, without the overlay of where it was supposed to be taking him. The dataset was its own argument. Every row was complete in itself. The 200,000 was the shape it would eventually take, but every batch of two thousand was already, in some sense, fully itself.

He was not sure this was Orthodox theology. He suspected Father Gheorghe might have some reservations about the application. But at 11pm in Brakpan, with the external hard drive blinking its patient green light, it was the closest thing to a sermon he had.

Chapter Five
The Bank Moves Slowly
"An institution is a collection of individual cautions."
I. David Stein's Reservations

The second call included a colleague. The third call was different. Ayanda had made, at some point in the third hour of that session, an expression that Langa had learned to recognise across the arc of his life in technical spaces: the face of a person who has just encountered something they had not known they were looking for. It arrived on a Tuesday, three weeks after the second call. He wrote a one-page disclosure. He stated what had happened with full accuracy — the fund, the trading strategy, the loss, the liquidation proceedings, the outcome. He did not editorialise. He did not know what I appreciate this meant, exactly. He knew it was not nothing. He filed it in the place where he kept the things he was not yet ready to examine.

Chapter Six
The Past Returns
"Guilt and credibility are not the same problem, though they feel identical from the inside."

Sipho Khumalo arrived on a Saturday morning in March, between the seven o'clock tutoring session and the one that started at nine. Langa heard the knock while he was erasing the whiteboard, and the particular rhythm of it — three knocks, a pause, two more — triggered a memory before his mind had consciously processed who it must be. "The time," Sipho said. "After."

He showed Sipho the dataset. Not because he had planned to, but because it was the only honest answer to the question of what he had done with the time after. He opened the spreadsheet. But this was not the same as before. Before, Langa had been performing a competence he had partly borrowed. Now he was reporting from a place he had actually been.

Langa looked at the spreadsheet. At the 112,000 rows. At the icon watching from its corner shelf.

"If this works," he said, "I become solvent."

Sipho nodded slowly. He looked at the word for a moment — solvent, and everything it contained about the distance between where Langa had been and where he was trying to get back to. He left. Langa stood in the doorway until the sound of his footsteps had gone down the path and turned onto Van Riebeeck Avenue and dissolved into the ordinary noise of a Brakpan Saturday. Then he closed the door and sat down in front of the laptop and annotated for six hours without stopping.

III. What Guilt Actually Is

He had thought for a long time that guilt was a feeling. That it would diminish when the feeling diminished — that you metabolised it, the way you metabolised other painful experiences, and what remained was scar tissue and a lesson and the possibility of moving on.

He had been wrong about this. Guilt was not a feeling. Guilt was a relationship. It required two parties and it changed whenever either party changed. Sipho had changed. He was not the same man who had sat across from Langa in 2021 with a bank statement and a handshake. He had absorbed the loss and restructured around it, and the Sipho who had sat in the lounge today was a man who had completed that process and come to see not the debt but the person.

This was, Langa thought, harder to carry than anger would have been. Anger he knew how to carry. Forgiveness — partial, adult, complicated, not absolution but acknowledgement — was a different weight entirely. It required him to be the same size as what had happened to him. Not smaller, not larger. The same size.

He worked until the load-shedding came at midnight. In the dark, he thought about solvency. He thought about what a word meant when it was the most ambitious thing you allowed yourself to want.

Outside, the amber sky. The generator down the street. The East Rand turning slowly under its own particular gravity, as it always had, as it always would.

Chapter Seven
The Server Problem
"The fragility of infrastructure is never about the hardware."
I. The Failover

The dataset had reached 149,622 rows on a Wednesday afternoon when the external hard drive made a sound. It was not a loud sound — just a rhythmic, metallic clicking, like a clock that had decided to count something other than time.

Langa froze. He did not move his mouse. He did not breathe.

149,622 rows. Fifty thousand from the target. Eight months of work. He sat in the amber light and listened to the clicking, and he felt the specific cold that comes when you realize the ground you are standing on is actually a trapdoor.

II. The Recovery Window

He spent forty minutes on data recovery forums. He found a procedure. The procedure required a Linux live environment, a second drive, and a tool called ddrescue that would attempt to clone the failing drive sector by sector, working around the bad blocks. He did not have a second drive. He had thirty-four rand in his bank account and a Capitec card that would decline on the thirty-fifth rand.

He did not call Thabiso, who would have helped. He did not call Mandla, who would have sourced a drive from somewhere in forty minutes. He sat with the logic of what was required and what he had and the space between those two things, which was a space he had spent two years learning to navigate without panic.

Then he opened his email and wrote to Nandi Dlamini.

The email was four lines. He described the situation without framing it as a crisis, because it was a crisis and he had learned that framing things as crises was an invitation for people to manage your crisis rather than help you solve it, and these were different things. He stated what he needed: temporary cloud storage of approximately 40GB, and, if she had access to institutional compute, a one-time write operation to move the recoverable data off the failing drive onto a secure location.

He sent it at 4:47pm.

She replied at 5:02pm: Call me.

III. The Call at Five

She answered on the first ring, which was unusual. He had learned her patterns over three months of intermittent contact — she typically let calls ring twice, a habit he suspected was deliberate, a small architecture of professional proportion. The first ring meant she had been waiting.

"Tell me the exact failure mode," she said, without greeting. This too was new — the removal of the professional wrapper. It was not rudeness. It was the register of someone who had decided that a situation did not require the performance of procedure.

He told her. She was quiet while he spoke. He heard, faintly, the sound of keys — she was typing while listening, which he recognised as its own form of attention.

"I can provision you a secure RMB cloud storage bucket," she said. "It will take me fifteen minutes. It is not formally sanctioned for external use, but I am classifying it as due diligence storage for the procurement evaluation, which is accurate." A brief pause. "Is that acceptable to you?"

"Yes."

"You will need to run the ddrescue clone to a local temp drive first, then transfer. Do you have a second physical drive?"

"No."

A silence. Not the silence of judgment — he had by now learned to distinguish the qualities of her silences, which was an intimate form of knowledge he had not sought and could not now unknow. It was the silence of active computation.

"There's a Incredible Connection in Boksburg East. They open until seven. A 1TB external is eleven hundred and ninety-nine rand. I will pay for it and you will settle it against the provisional licensing advance when the agreement is signed." She said this matter-of-factly, in the same voice she used for data architecture. "Alternatively, you can decline and I will find another option. Either is fine."

Langa looked at the icon on the shelf. The gold Pantocrator. The patient watchfulness of a face that had been looking at the same corner of this room for eight months.

"I will settle it against the advance," he said.

"Good. I am sending you the Bolt voucher now. Go directly to the store. Do not attempt to run any write operations on the failing drive until you have the second drive in hand."

"I know," he said.

"I know you know," she said. "I am saying it anyway."

There was something in the last exchange that was different from the ones before. He could not name it precisely. He filed it in the place where he kept the things he was not yet ready to examine, but that place was filling up, and he understood that he would need to examine it soon. After the deal was closed. When the ground was solid.

He picked up his jacket. He opened the door. The East Rand evening was warm and copper and indifferent to the urgency of a man's particular Wednesday, and he walked down Sarel Cilliers Street to the road where the Bolt was already waiting — a white Corolla, a different driver this time, a young woman who drove in silence and whose silence was its own form of grace.

IV. The Recovery

He recovered 147,309 rows. He lost two thousand three hundred and thirteen — a week's work, spread across twelve batches, gone to the corrupted partition with the finality of things that are truly gone.

On the fifth day he uploaded the complete recovered dataset to the secure server Nandi had provided and sent her a confirmation email. He did not ask for a follow-up. He did not explain the loss. He simply stated the integrity of the corpus and the final row count.

He read the email three times in Thabiso's café on a Saturday, a full week later — the silence of the intervening days having been a particular form of work he hadn't yet learned to automate. Nandi's reply was shorter than usual.

He thought about Sipho. About the word solvent. About 147,309 rows that existed because he had not stopped. About a woman in Meadowlands, Zone 8, who had been given her father's precision and her mother's read of a room, and who had written something in a brown notebook on a shelf in an office in Sandton that he had not read and might never read and would think about anyway.

He opened batch forty-seven.

Outside, the last of the Highveld light came through the café window at the specific angle that turned everything it touched to gold — the keyboards, the screens, the dust on Thabiso's laminator, the face-brick walls of Tsakane — and Langa Ndimande sat inside it and worked, as he had learned to work, without grasping for the next moment, without mourning the last, awake only to the row in front of him and the one that would come after, and after, and after.

Chapter Eight
The Dataset Begins to Speak
"Data is not neutral. It is a record of what happened to people."
I. What the Rows Contained

He had been annotating for so long that the material had ceased to be material and had become atmosphere. The SENS announcements, the equity filings, the macro releases — they passed through him the way music passes through a room, changing the quality of the air without requiring deliberate attention. He processed. He tagged. He moved on. This was the discipline.

Then, on a Thursday evening in what had become the deep middle of the project — 163,000 rows, the dataset past its equator, the far shore finally visible if you squinted — something interrupted the discipline.

It was a single sentence in a SENS announcement from a mid-tier mining house in the Northern Cape, filed on a Tuesday in April 2018. The announcement concerned an operational review. The sentence read: Following a strategic assessment of the Prieska operations, the board has resolved to place the operation into care and maintenance effective 1 June 2018, resulting in the retrenchment of approximately 1,400 employees.

He had read ten thousand sentences like this. He had read this exact construction — the passive voice of corporate decision, the sanitised grammar of collective harm — hundreds of times. He had tagged them all with the same efficiency: Negative. Material. Event class: Operational restructuring.

But this time he stopped. He did not know why, precisely, except that the number 1,400 had made contact with something that the previous numbers had not. He sat with the sentence. He thought about Prieska — a small town in the Karoo, dependent on a single mine the way certain towns were dependent on a single lung — and he thought about 1,400 people receiving the notification that their employment had been placed into care and maintenance, which was the corporate language for a death that had not yet been formally announced.

He looked at his annotation: Negative. Material.

It was correct. By every technical measure of his taxonomy, it was exactly correct.

And yet.

II. The Problem of Negative

For the first time in 163,000 rows, Langa Ndimande found himself unable to apply his own annotation without reservation. The word negative in his taxonomy meant: this announcement is associated with declining equity value, with market pessimism, with instruments that would likely fall in price in the trading sessions that followed. This was what the model would learn. This was what the model was supposed to learn.

But for the 1,400 people in Prieska, the announcement was not negative sentiment. It was the end of a town's economy. It was children being told that the school fees would not be paid next term. It was a particular kind of silence descending on a high street. Negative was the smallest possible word for what the filing contained, and the smallest possible word was the word the machine would learn.

He opened a new column in the spreadsheet. He called it NOTES. He had never used it before. He typed, in the Prieska row: 1,400 jobs. Town of approx. 8,000 people. This is not a market event. It is a community event recorded in market language.

He stared at the note for a while. It would not go into the model. The model would not read it. The model would read the tag — Negative. Material. Operational restructuring — and learn that language of this kind predicted price movements of this kind, and that would be the entirety of what the machine understood about Prieska.

This was, he knew, not a problem unique to his dataset. It was not even a problem he could solve within his dataset. It was the foundational problem of the enterprise he had given eight months of his life to — the question of what exactly you were teaching a machine when you taught it to read financial language, and what you were not teaching it, and whether the gap between those two things mattered.

He thought about what a model trained entirely on this data would know. It would know fear. It would know the grammar of collapse, the vocabulary of restructuring, the syntax of institutions protecting themselves from consequences they had created. It would know South African economic history as a sequence of negative sentiment events.

It would not know Prieska. It would know the price of Prieska.

III. The NOTES Column

He kept annotating. He did not stop — stopping was not available to him, not at 163,000 rows, not with the RMB evaluation underway, not with the drive's replacement still to be settled against an advance that had not yet been formalised. But he began using the NOTES column, and what accumulated there over the following weeks was something he had not planned and could not classify.

A state capture case — the looting of a pension fund that had belonged to mineworkers in Mpumalanga. He tagged the announcement: Negative. Material. Regulatory/Legal. In the NOTES column he wrote: These are retirement savings. The people who owned them will not recover them.

A municipal bond default in a small Free State town. Negative. Material. Credit event. NOTES: This municipality supplies water to 40,000 people. A credit event is not the correct category for what happens when water infrastructure fails.

A corporate earnings beat — rare good news, a JSE-listed retailer reporting above-forecast revenue on the back of Black Friday sales. Positive. Material. Earnings. NOTES: These earnings come from a quarter in which South African consumer debt reached a twenty-year high. The retailer sells on credit. The positive and the negative are the same event from different angles.

The NOTES column would never be read by the model. It might never be read by anyone. It was, Langa understood, a form of annotation he was performing for himself — a record of what the dataset contained that the dataset could not say about itself. He was building two things simultaneously: the corpus that a machine would learn from, and the marginal commentary that preserved what the machine would not be taught.

He did not know what to do with this understanding. He filed it in the place where he kept the things he was not yet ready to act on, which was becoming a crowded place.

Chapter Nine
Saturdays in Tsakane
"The township economy runs on different laws. Not lesser ones. Different ones."
I. The Ecology of a Saturday

The car wash operated from an unpaved lot beside the Shell garage on the main road, run by four teenagers who had divided the labour with an efficiency that no MBA programme would have been able to improve on. One took payment. One pre-rinsed. Two worked the soap and the chamois in a sequence calibrated by repetition to take exactly fourteen minutes per car. He watched them from the plastic chair outside Thabiso's café while he waited for his download to complete. The older of the four — he couldn't have been more than seventeen, with the self-possessed ease of someone who had discovered that competence was its own kind of authority — caught his eye once and looked away with the specific indifference of a young person who has assessed an adult and found nothing requiring acknowledgment. III. The Drawing Set

On his way back to the Bolt rank he stopped at a street stall that sold soap, airtime, and a miscellany of small things arranged on a folding table with the democratic abundance of a stall that had decided everything was potentially useful to someone. There was a drawing set — a child's set, twelve wax crayons in a cardboard box printed with a giraffe, retail price forty-nine rand — and he picked it up before he had consciously decided to.

He thought about the drawing. The rectangle head. Come home soon.

He paid forty-nine rand, which was a material portion of what he had left until the tutoring parents paid on Saturday. He put the drawing set in his jacket pocket. It made a small square weight against his chest on the Bolt ride back to Brakpan, the East Rand unreeling through the window in its habitual late-afternoon amber, the mine dumps on the horizon like the remains of something enormous that had been consumed and was still waiting for someone to acknowledge what it had cost.

He would send it to Amahle with Zanele. He would not include a note, because any note he wrote would be about himself rather than about her, and she did not need that. The drawing set could speak for itself. It could say: I saw this and thought of you. That was enough. That was, possibly, more than enough.

He looked at the mine dumps until they were behind him. He did not look at them again.

Chapter Ten
The Procurement Meeting
"Institutions don't negotiate with people. They negotiate with positions."
I. The Room

The meeting was in person. This had not been specified in the calendar invite — it had simply said Dataset Procurement Review — RMB Sandton, and Langa had assumed another video call until Nandi's message arrived the Friday before: They want you in the room. Is that feasible?

He had replied: Yes.

Then he had spent the weekend doing the calculation of feasibility, which involved: the taxi to the Wanderers rank, the Gautrain to Sandton, the walk to the RMB building, and the question of what to wear, which was a question that had once been automatic and was now a question with consequences. II. The Questions

The room had a glass wall overlooking Sandton and a conference table that seated twelve comfortably. Six people were present: David Stein, Ayanda Mokoena, two lawyers whose names Langa did not fully catch, and two executives he had not met — a Chief Data Officer named Priya Naidoo who asked questions with the surgical brevity of someone whose time was a finite and audited resource, and a man introduced only as Willem from Risk, who said nothing for the first forty minutes and then said one thing that changed the shape of everything.

"Exclusivity in what sense?" Langa said.

Standard. The word did a great deal of work in that sentence. It meant: we have done this before, with other people, and they agreed. It meant: resistance is the deviation, not the norm. "I'll need to review the full terms with counsel before I can respond to that," he said.

III. What David Stein Said in the Corridor

The meeting ended with the formal pleasantries of a process that had not concluded but had arrived at a pause. Nandi walked him to the elevator. They did not speak in the corridor. In the elevator she said: "You handled the exclusivity question well." He did not ask her what poorly would have looked like. He suspected it would have looked like him agreeing immediately, which he had wanted to do, for a moment, because the room had been arranged to make agreement feel like the natural thing.

In the lobby, David Stein caught up with him at the door. He was carrying his jacket over his arm, as though he had arranged to be leaving at the same moment by coincidence, which Langa understood was not a coincidence.

"You need a lawyer," David Stein said. Not unkindly. With the matter-of-factness of a man who had watched independent operators lose things they should have kept because they had not understood what they were giving away.

"I know," Langa said.

"Exclusivity on a dataset of this nature, for five years, is not nothing. Make sure you understand what you are licensing versus what you are selling. They are not the same transaction."

He put on his jacket. He was, Langa realised, telling him something he was not supposed to tell him, in the specific way of an old-school banker who still believed that the integrity of a deal required both parties to understand what they were agreeing to. It was an act of professional honour dressed as corridor small talk, and Langa received it as such.

"Thank you," he said.

David Stein nodded once, the nod of a man who had not said anything and therefore had nothing to be thanked for, and walked to the parking level.

Langa walked out into the Sandton afternoon. The sun was high and indifferent and the street was full of the purposeful movement of people whose day was structured differently from his. He found the Gautrain entrance and descended into the cool underground and sat in a seat facing backward, watching Sandton recede, thinking about the word exclusivity and what it contained, and what the NOTES column contained, and whether a machine trained on a dataset it owned entirely would learn the same things as a machine trained on a dataset that still had a person attached to it who remembered Prieska.

Chapter Eleven
The Quiet Discipline
"Guard the mind. Not from the world — but from your own noise."
I. Sunday Morning

He woke before the alarm on Sunday and lay still for a few minutes in the way he had trained himself to begin days that required something from him — not the procurement meeting Sundays, not the tutoring Sundays, but the Sundays that belonged to nothing external and were therefore the most demanding of all. The light through the curtain was the pale gold of an early Highveld winter, thin and specific, the light that arrived before the day had committed to itself.

He rose. He made rooibos — the gas had run out three days ago and he was waiting for the next tutoring payment to replace the cylinder, so the kettle ran on the electricity that the municipality had not yet remembered to charge him for. He carried the mug to the room with the dataset and the icon and stood there for a moment, as he sometimes did, letting the room be what it was: the site of the work, and the site of the practice, and the site of his own particular version of a life that had not gone as planned but had become, he was slowly beginning to understand, exactly as complex as it needed to be.

He lit the beeswax candle. He opened the prayer book. He read the Sunday morning office in the way Father Konstantinos had shown him — not as recitation but as attention, each line considered, the meaning not extracted but inhabited. The Trisagion. The Our Father. The troparia for the day. He read slowly, the way he annotated, one unit at a time, without rushing toward completion.

Outside, Brakpan was quiet. Sunday had its own sound — the dogs that barked differently on Sundays, the absence of the weekday traffic on Van Riebeeck Avenue, the occasional car moving with the deliberate pace of people going somewhere that required them to be dressed. Somewhere a church bell — not Orthodox, something Dutch Reformed or Pentecostal — rang its plain summons to whoever was listening.

He listened to it finish. Then he returned to the prayer book.

II. The Question of Ambition

He had been sitting with a question for three weeks, since the Sandton meeting, and the question had the quality of things that grew rather than diminished the longer you sat with them. It was this: whether what he was building was a service or a surrender.

The Orthodox tradition he had been slowly entering had a specific relationship to wealth and ambition — not the Protestant relationship, which was essentially optimistic about their compatibility with a well-ordered soul, and not the contemptuous relationship of a certain kind of monasticism, which simply evacuated the question by removing the self from the world where wealth operated. The Orthodox relationship was more unsettling than either. It acknowledged the legitimacy of material life while insisting, relentlessly, on the examination of motive. Not what you are building, but why. Not what the dataset is worth, but what you are worth without it.

He thought about the exclusivity clause. He thought about what it would mean to deliver 200,000 rows of annotated South African financial history to an institution that would use them to build a model that would, in some downstream sense, make that institution better at predicting the events the dataset described. Better at predicting, for instance, the Prieska closure. Better at positioning ahead of the retrenchment announcements. Better at profiting from the grammar of national anxiety that the NOTES column had been trying, inadequately, to acknowledge.

Was this a service? Or was it the completion of a transaction that the system had been arranging since before he had begun, in which his labour — his specific, solitary, painstaking labour — became an instrument of the same financial architecture that had, in its previous engagement with him, taken R4.7 million from people who could not afford to lose it?

He did not have an answer. He was not sure an answer was available. He was sure that the question needed to stay alive — that closing it prematurely, in either direction, would cost him something more important than the deal.

III. What Amahle Asked

She called at half past ten, which was unusual. Zanele handled the children's communication with him — relaying messages, managing logistics, maintaining the architecture of a separated family that still required its members to be parents even when they were not together. A direct call from Amahle meant either that Zanele had sanctioned it or that Amahle had simply decided, with the sovereign confidence of an eight-year-old who has assessed a situation and found it manageable, to pick up the phone.

"Daddy."

"Amahle." He felt the particular compression in his chest that her voice on the phone produced, different from her voice in person — more fragile in the phone, more exposed to the distance between them. "How are you?"

"I got the drawing set." A pause. "Thank you."

"You're welcome."

"Daddy, are computers smarter than people?"

He sat with the question for a moment. Not performing consideration — actually considering, the way the question deserved. He thought about the model he was about to train on 200,000 rows of human pain and human language and human error. He thought about what the model would be able to do that he could not, and what it would never be able to do that he could do without thinking.

"Computers are good at remembering things," he said. "People are good at forgetting."

A silence. He could hear her breathing, the small sounds of a child considering an adult's answer with the seriousness it deserved.

"Why is forgetting good?" she said.

He looked at the icon. At the candle. At the prayer book open on the shelf. At the external hard drive with its 178,000 rows of what a country remembered about itself, and all the things the NOTES column had tried, in private, to say about that memory.

"Because without forgetting," he said, "you can't start again."

Amahle was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "Okay. Bye, Daddy." And she was gone.

He held the phone for a long time after. Then he put it down and opened the prayer book and read the afternoon office, and when he was done he sat in the Sunday quiet of Brakpan and allowed himself, for one hour, to do nothing at all.

Chapter Twelve
The Training Run
"You do not know what you have built until it begins to predict."
I. The 200,000th Row

He finished the dataset on a Wednesday at 11:47pm. He knew it was Wednesday because the load-shedding schedule had been on the kitchen counter and he had been working against it — racing the next slot, which was scheduled for midnight — and he had cleared the final batch at 11:47 with thirteen minutes to spare, and then the power had gone anyway at 11:52 because the schedule was aspirational rather than binding, and he had sat in the dark with the laptop on battery, the screen the only light in the house, and looked at the completed file.

200,412 rows. The extra 412 were from a batch he had not been able to stop himself from completing once the end was visible.

He did not celebrate. He had learned, from the previous time — from the investment company, from the algorithm, from the dashboard that had looked like Bloomberg Terminal — what celebrations before the outcome could cost. He sat with the completed file in the dark and allowed himself to feel, briefly and without elaboration, the thing that the work of two years entitled him to feel: that this was real, and that it was his, and that nobody had given it to him.

Then he opened his email and sent the final corpus to the RMB cloud bucket.

Then the battery died.

He sat in complete darkness for the remaining hour of the load-shedding slot, and he thought about nothing in particular, which was the most restful thing he had done in two years.

II. Cloud Time

He rented the GPU instance on a Friday, using a cloud compute platform recommended by Ayanda — she had sent him a credit voucher, provisioned from an RMB developer account, with a note that said: For the training run. This is not a formal arrangement. Consider it due diligence preparation. He understood this to mean that Ayanda had spent her own allowance. He filed this in the place where he kept the things that mattered and said nothing about them.

The instance cost forty-one US dollars per hour. He had budgeted eight hours. The fine-tuning run on a model of this scale, on a corpus of this size, should take six hours on the hardware he'd provisioned — he had calculated this from benchmarks on a community forum at 2am on a Tuesday, cross-referencing against three separate papers on similar-scale financial model fine-tuning. He had double-checked the calculation four times. He was, he acknowledged to himself, checking it the way a man checks something when what he is really doing is postponing the moment of commitment.

He committed. He launched the run at 11pm on a Friday and watched the first log lines scroll across the terminal with the particular held-breath attention of someone watching something begin that cannot be un-begun.

EPOCH1/4 — STEP 847 — LOSS: 2.341 — LR: 0.000023
EPOCH1/4 — STEP 1204 — LOSS: 2.108 — LR: 0.000021
EPOCH2/4 — STEP 2891 — LOSS: 1.687 — LR: 0.000018
EPOCH3/4 — STEP 4102 — LOSS: 1.203 — LR: 0.000012
EPOCH4/4 — STEP 5446 — LOSS: 0.891 — LR: 0.000006

The loss fell the way things fell when they were falling in the right direction — not smoothly, with the small perturbations and recoveries of a system learning, but falling nonetheless, the numbers moving in the direction numbers were supposed to move when the underlying structure was sound. He watched the screen from the kitchen table with a mug of rooibos going cold beside him and the load-shedding schedule on the counter and the icon watching from the other room, and he felt, for the first time since the liquidation, that he was watching something he had built do something it was supposed to do.

III. What the Model Had Learned

The evaluation results came back at 6am on a Saturday, having run through the night while he slept in three-hour segments, waking each time to check the logs and returning to the mattress when the numbers were still moving in the right direction.

Overall accuracy on held-out test data: 71.4%. For a first fine-tuning run on a niche corpus, with a base model not originally trained for financial tasks, this was respectable. It was not state-of-the-art. It was proof of concept, which was what it needed to be.

But the disaggregated results were what stopped him.

Prediction accuracy on negative-sentiment events: 84.3%.

Prediction accuracy on positive-sentiment events: 54.1%.

The model was dramatically better at predicting bad news than good. It had learned, from 200,000 rows of South African financial history, to expect the worst with a precision that exceeded its ability to recognise improvement by thirty percentage points. It was not biased in the technical sense — there was no error in the training procedure, no flaw in the data pipeline. It had simply learned what the data contained. And what the data contained, it turned out, was a country's relationship with its own uncertainty, recorded in the language of markets, over fifteen years.

He had built a machine that understood economic pessimism with the fluency of a native speaker. It had learned the emotional structure of a country. The NOTES column had tried to say this in human language. The loss curve had said it in mathematics.

He sat with this for a long time.

Then he wrote the evaluation summary and attached it to an email to Nandi. He did not editorialize. He presented the numbers and let them speak. At the bottom of the email, in a postscript that he wrote and deleted twice before leaving in, he added: The asymmetry in the accuracy figures is not a flaw. It is a finding. I think it tells us something about the data that is worth discussing before the licensing terms are finalised.

He sent it. He looked at the icon. He opened the NOTES column and read through it once, from the beginning — all the things the machine had learned from, and all the things he had written beside them that the machine had not been taught. Then he closed the file and made tea and sat by the window and watched Brakpan wake up, the amber light coming in off the East Rand in the particular way it always came, neither promising nor withholding, simply arriving, the way light arrived, the way the sun arrived, the way his name said it should.

Chapter Thirteen
The Offer
"The moment before a decision is the last moment in which everything is still possible."
I. David Stein's Call

David Stein called on a Thursday at 3pm. Not Nandi. Not Ayanda. David Stein, who had told him to get a lawyer in a Sandton corridor three weeks ago and who had not communicated since, calling now from what sounded, from the ambient texture of the audio, like a car.

"We have reviewed the evaluation summary," David Stein said. "Including the postscript." A pause in which Langa heard the particular silence of a man choosing his words with the care of someone who has spent decades in rooms where words had consequences. "The committee has approved a formal offer. I am authorised to convey the terms verbally before the written document is prepared. Do you have a moment?"

"Yes," Langa said. He was in the kitchen. He moved to the table and sat down.

The number David Stein said was a number Langa had not allowed himself to imagine, because imagining numbers before they existed was the error that had made his first company possible and his first company's collapse inevitable. He had spent two years learning not to imagine numbers. He had apparently not imagined this one correctly enough, because when David Stein said it, the room changed.

Not dramatically. The linoleum remained the linoleum. The kettle was still the kettle with the broken temperature indicator. The icon watched from the other room with its accustomed patience. But something in the proportions of the space shifted, the way rooms shift when they contain new information about what they are the site of.

"The conditions," David Stein continued, "are as discussed. Full data rights. Exclusivity for five years. First right of refusal on extensions. And — this was added following your postscript — a research credit in any published work derived from the model, acknowledging the corpus originator."

The research credit. Someone had read the postscript and understood what it was asking for.

"I need to review this with counsel," Langa said. His voice was level. He had rehearsed this sentence, in the abstract, many times.

"Of course," David Stein said. "The written offer will arrive Monday. You have fourteen days." Another pause. "For what it's worth — and I am not speaking in an official capacity — the postscript was the right thing to include. It changed the conversation internally."

The line went quiet. David Stein had ended the call the way he ended everything, without ceremony, having said exactly what needed to be said and no more.

II. What the Number Contained

He did not call anyone. He sat at the kitchen table for forty minutes and thought about what the number contained. It contained: the Brakpan mortgage settled, which meant a house that was actually his rather than a house Absa had not yet decided to reclaim. It contained: Amahle's school fees for three years, four if he was careful. Sibusiso's, too. It contained: a lawyer — a real one, not the free legal advice available to people in his income bracket, which was advice calibrated to their circumstances rather than their interests. It contained the possibility of a company. Not the previous company, not the algorithm and the dashboard and the borrowed confidence, but a real company, built on the thing he now actually knew how to do, which was build things of genuine worth in the dark and alone.

It also contained the exclusivity clause, and everything the exclusivity clause meant about Prieska, and the NOTES column, and the machine that now predicted bad news with the fluency of a country that had been practicing bad news for fifteen years.

He thought about Thabiso's question: You're building a business for banks. Banks don't build businesses for people like us.

He thought about his own answer: I'm building it because it is the only thing I know how to build right now that is worth buying.

Both of these were true. That they were simultaneously true was not a contradiction he could resolve by accepting or declining the offer. It was a contradiction he would carry regardless of what he decided, because it was not a contradiction in the deal. It was a contradiction in the world.

III. Daveyton at Dusk

He booked a Bolt at 4pm and told the driver Daveyton without a specific destination, which confused the driver enough that Langa simply named a café he had been to twice — a small place on the main road that had plastic chairs outside and sold fat cakes and good filter coffee and had, on both previous occasions, been almost entirely empty in the late afternoon.

It was not empty. A family was celebrating something — a birthday, a graduation, some occasion that had brought eight people to four pushed-together tables, with a cake in a box and a child in her best dress who kept standing on her chair to see the cake better. The ordinary abundance of it — the noise, the laughter, the child's dress, the cake — occupied the space in a way that made Langa's situation feel, for a moment, like the least important thing happening in Daveyton on a Thursday afternoon, which was possibly accurate.

He ordered coffee. He opened the laptop. The model was still running on the cloud instance — he had set it to continue refining on additional validation batches, using the remaining credit. He watched the log lines scroll. The loss figures continued their patient, incremental descent toward accuracy. The machine was learning, right now, from what he had given it to learn from. It was becoming, with each gradient step, slightly better at understanding the financial language of a country he had been living inside his whole life.

Outside, the Highveld evening was beginning its approach — that slow, amber-pink transition that the East Rand performed each day without variation or diminishment, the sky going through its inventory of golds and the mine dumps holding their ground on the horizon and the street filling up with the end-of-day movement of people going toward whatever was waiting for them at home.

The load-shedding began at 6pm. The café's lights went out. The family at the birthday table produced, from somewhere, a phone flashlight that they pointed at the ceiling to fill the room with diffuse light, and the child in the best dress thought this was wonderful and laughed, and the laughter was the sound of someone for whom darkness was not a problem but a texture, one more thing the day contained.

Langa's screen went black as the cloud instance lost its API connection in the same moment.

He sat in the dark of a Daveyton café with a cold coffee and a decision that was not due for fourteen days and an offer written in numbers he had not allowed himself to imagine and a NOTES column full of things no machine would ever read, and he thought about his daughter's question: Are computers smarter than people?

And his answer: Computers are good at remembering things. People are good at forgetting.

And her follow-up, the one that had stayed with him through everything since: Why is forgetting good?

Because without forgetting, you cannot start again.

He sat with that. He sat with the dark. The child at the birthday table was still laughing. Outside, Daveyton moved through the load-shedding the way it always had — not stopping, not complaining, simply continuing, adjusting, finding the way through with what it had, which was what it had always done and what it would go on doing long after the grid was fixed and long after the deal was signed or not signed and long after Langa Ndimande had made his decision and become whatever he was going to become.

The power came back at 6:47pm. The café lights returned. His screen woke. The log lines resumed their scroll.

He closed the laptop. He finished his cold coffee. He watched the child in the best dress get the first slice of cake, which she held in both hands like something she had been promised and had almost stopped believing in.

He did not decide anything. He let the evening be the evening.

Tomorrow he would call a lawyer. After that, he would see.
